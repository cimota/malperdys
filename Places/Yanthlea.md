# Atlantis
Y’a-nthlea is no shining Atlantis of marble and sunlight, but a drowned inheritance of black basalt terraces, cyclopean sea-walls, and echoing halls half-flooded by the slow pulse of the abyss, where the last memory of human kingship has curdled into something older and stranger. Beneath its green-dark vaults dwell the Deep One hybrids, neither wholly man nor wholly sea-thing, a people of pallid eyes, lacquer-black hair, and cold, watchful grace, who move through saltwater courts and barnacled sanctuaries with the solemnity of a fallen nobility. They remember the surface as a place of exile, theft, and fading blood, while Y’a-nthlea itself endures as a kingdom of mingled decadence and biological dread, rich with pearl idols, drowned archives, and ancestral songs chanted in voices that thrum like whale-calls through the deep. Here beauty and corruption are indivisible, and every child born of the city carries in their veins the old covenant between humankind and the devouring sea.

The sorceries of Y’a-nthlea are not the bright and formal arts of men, nor even the fevered blasphemies of landbound cults, but tidal workings, moon-pulled and blood-deep, learned through dream, breeding, and remembrance. Their magicians awaken inheritances sleeping in flesh and seawater: they can still the pulse of a storm, call pale lights into the trenches, thicken the sea into clinging black pressure, or sing to the bones of drowned things and make them stir. Much of their power lies in transformation and summons, in the slow unmaking of human limits. 

And they themselves are terrible in their beauty, cold as polished ivory, dark-eyed, silver-skinned in the lampglow of the deep, with faces almost perfectly human until one notices the unblinking patience, and the hint of something ancient and predatory behind every elegant gesture. In them there is the same uncanny majesty once feared in the Fomorians of old legend: not mere monsters, but the memory of a prior claim upon the world, a race touched by the sea, storm, and under-earth, whose beauty was never warm or kindly, but the beauty of a wave before it breaks, of a moonlit reef, of a king or queen who might embrace you like a lover and drag you smiling into the black.
